SPITE will never be a fun game, that is not in its nature. But still technically a game, I'm referring to it more as an experience. The game is about the weakness of a dying person. How energy leaves the body, but the will to survive doesn't.

A soldier on deaths' door, fleeing from the battlefield, is hunted by beasts.

If one dies, it isn't from a lack of health points, but from the loss of energy. So, hope you can stand up before you can't. But beasts are just beasts, and you just have to survive.

Keeping it very brief, the feeling of fighting your bodies need for rest, knowing you won't wake up from it again, stems from a personal experience. Before this I never really understood that age-old scene of someone's life slipping away, and some else slaps them in the face: "Please stay awake!".

Originally the project started in Game Maker 2, then moved over into the Phaser Game Engine. It was in 2.5D with pixelated simple monochrome sprites and focus on a limited color pallet. As with all my other projects, mostly chosen because I don't particularly like making sprites.

As mentioned there was no "health" system, just stamina.

  • Attacking, guarding, and being knocked down, will deplete stamina.
  • If that stamina reaches zero you will die.
  • Getting hit by enemies or running while exhausted will knock you down.

Packs of beasts, prowling the edges of the battlefield, would make hunt for the player. Encircling and knocking them down with every hit. I had a system designed that gave the pack and the individual beasts basic 2-dimensional psychological profiles (sounds more impressive than it is).

Now, combining this with the slow paced combat model in which every move carries a risky stamina investment and execution time, leaving you open to attacks. Every fight is a slow motion game of chess about exploiting their fear of death or, making oneself uninteresting as an energy investment a.k.a., boredom.

I had to write some real funky code to make a custom sprite system that translates player movement to pixel perfect aperiodic animation speeds. But that made the animations real crisp.
